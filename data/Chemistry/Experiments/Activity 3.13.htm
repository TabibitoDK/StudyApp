Activity 3.13

Determine the empirical formula of copper (II) oxide.

Aim: To determine the empirical formula of copper (II) oxide.

Materials: Water, copper (II) oxide powder, zinc granules, 1.0 mol dm–3 hydrochloric acid, wooden splinter, and cotton buds

Apparatus: Boiling tube, rubber stoppers, rubber tube, 12 cm glass tube, 10 cm glass tube, spirit lamp, retort stand with clamp, wooden block, electronic balance, and spatula

Procedure:

1

The mass of 12 cm glass tube is weighted using an electronic balance and recorded.

2

Some copper (II) oxide powder is put into the glass tube. The wooden splinter is used to move copper (II) oxide powder to the middle of the glass tube. The mass of the glass tube together with its contents is weighted and recorded.

3

2/3 of the boiling tube is filled with water.

4

The boiling tube is closed with a rubber stopper that has a 12 cm glass tube. The boiling tube is clamped onto the retort stand.

5

A few zinc granules is inserted into another boiling tube. 1.0 mol dm–3 hydrochloric acid is added into the boiling tube until it is 1/3 full.

6

The boiling tube is closed with a rubber stopper that has a 10 cm glass tube. The boiling tube is clamped onto the other retort stand.

7

The glass tube that contains copper (II) oxide powder is connected as shown in Figure 3.13.

8

The hydrogen gas is let to flow for 10 seconds by allowing the air bubbles to be released in the water before starting the heating process.

9

Copper (II) oxide is heated using a spirit lamp with a continuous flow of hydrogen gas through the glass tube.

10

The heating is stopped when the black color of copper (II) oxide completely turned brown.

11

A continuous flow of hydrogen gas is kept until the glass tube is cooled back to room temperature.

12

The glass tube that contains brown powder is removed. Water drops at the end of the glass tube are eliminated with a cotton bud.

13

The mass of the glass tube together with its contents is measured and recorded.

14

The heating, cooling and weighing processes is repeated from steps 9 to 13 until a constant mass reading is obtained.

15

The constant mass is recorded in Table 3.5.

Discussion:

1. What is the purpose of using zinc granules and hydrochloric acid in this activity?

To produce hydrogen gas

2. Why does the hydrogen gas need to flow continuously for a while before starting the heating process?

To remove air in the glass tube

3. The hydrogen gas is allowed to flow until the product of heating is at room temperature in step 11. Why?

To prevent air from entering the apparatus because oxygen in air will oxidize the hot copper back to copper (II) oxide

4. Why do the heating, cooling, and weighing processes need to be repeated until a constant mass is obtained?

To ensure that all copper (II) oxide are completely changed to copper
